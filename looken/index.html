Looken

Responsive single page app

Role: UX Designer & Front-end Developer

Introduction

I had the pleasure of working at Looken, a startup based in Las Vegas, for a little over 4 months. The Looken platform has as its main focus a search engine that accrues multiple online deal providers (Groupon, Travelzoo, Amazon Local, etc) and adds some social elements (follow, like, comment, etc) to engage the user.

Defining location

Knowing the user’s location is crucial in populating the site with relevant deals. The challenge, in this case, was elaborating a seamless process to capture a user’s exact location, given that the phone’s GPS for some reason failed to acquire this data. I went through some user flows and ended up with the following wireframes.

First approach was to rely on GPS then if that failed a smart search box appear

Second approach – I went a little more in detail as to find a location without using a phone’s GPS. I highlighted certain “hints” that would allow the user to see if a location was set or not.

Wireframing Deals

While I believe a good visual composition is pleasing to the eye and can win over users, I also strongly believe there is far more significance in a useful interface than there is in a pretty one. While at Looken, I managed to wireframe various ‘finds’ (or deals), mobile being the focus most of the time.

Prototyping using Bootstrap

A month into the job, I was asked to make some prototypes. I used the bootstrap framework as requested by my team. A few weeks later, I was learning different JS frameworks to help with the production code.

Where to focus

There were three main components to the application: search, e-commerce and some lingering social features. Almost at the end of my stay, I began studying and researching similar platforms to find where our focus needed to be. I knew everyone expected to release this behemoth of an app altogether, but as time passed I had grown wary of our app’s utility. While I studied Kayak, Priceline, Cheaptickets and other similar useful platforms, I began obsessing over utility and search as a whole. “Deals, products, events among other things need to be targeted,” I thought. The fact of the matter is we needed something “superior” and ways to test our hypotheses. But sadly, there wasn’t time for that.

From time to time, after finishing working on something, I still sketch and prototype certain ways I could have done things better. This is how I envisioned Looken (of course just visually)

Conclusion

As a designer I saw potential in this startup. I knew it’d be a huge project to work on, for the magnitude of things involved. Ironically, the biggest problem lied in the amount of things to be designed and implemented [by a single person], as well as our inability to push updates on time. I worked in a team of 5 people where I was the only designer. This meant I was in charge of creating wireframes and graphic work for every part of the site. Somewhere along the line, I found myself more involved in front-end development than user experience, and this I felt was counterproductive for both the startup and myself. My stay in the startup was short-lived however the things I learned are invaluable.
