Dramafever

Redesigning an iPad application
(Personal Project)

Role: UX/UI Designer

About a year a go, while searching for sites offering foreign shows with subtitles, I came across Dramafever. Navigating the website was easy, practical and above all enjoyable, so I decided to give it a try. A few hours into it, I felt compelled to download the app for my iPad – this is where the problems began. The app was buggy so it’d crash from time to time, leaving me annoyed and dissatisfied with my experience. The interface was also somewhat outdated, and this pop up asking me if I wanted to turn off the ads by paying a monthly fee did not help either. The overall mobile user experience was substantially different than on the web. As a user I decided to change a few things to ameliorate this application.

Resuming Videos to Resume Experience

With constant crashes – which should not occur at all – the least I expect is some kind of help to get me back to where I was. For instance, Youtube automatically resumes a video if by any chance the browser crashes

While Dramafever made some efforts to include a way to get back to a video, the app was still buggy and sometimes it would leave empty the “Continue Watching” section. My approach to this problem was to simply make the video to be resumed larger therefore more notorious and add a cancel button on the top right corner if the user didn’t intend on resuming it.

Removing the Unnecessary

Although I understand the motive behind a pop up reminding me every time I log into the app that I can turn off advertisement by paying a monthly fee, I still find this redundancy unnecessary. With that in mind, I decided to approach this problem with a less aggressive solution.

A button to “Turn off ads” is a bit more suggestive in my opinion. This button is ubiquitous throughout the app and it serves as a constant reminder unlike a pop up that appears and disappears during a session.

Restructuring a show's page

While attempting to transfer an equally enjoyable user experience to mobile devices, Dramafever neglected some differences between web and mobile interfaces. Shrinking an interface, I mean, to fit a web-based layout into a mobile device does not assure the same experience.

To go places and do things that have never been done before – that’s what living is all about.

Most of the work I did here were ‘aesthetic touches.’ There isn’t much space to work with on mobile devices, so maximizing usage of space is critical. I moved things around to highlight what is important (“the latest episode of a show per say”) and made sure navigation was accessible and intuitive by including tabs and a hamburger icon on the top left corner.

Takeaway

Dramafever was an interesting personal project for its size. The app does not contain every social element included on the web – which is understandable – therefore it must concentrate on the smallest user interactions to optimize user experience. By redesigning certain aspects of this app, I hope to accentuate the importance of a good UI and bring attention to small user tasks.
