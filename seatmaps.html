Seat Maps for Flights

The task assigned to us was clear: revamp seat maps, sell more premium seats and incorporate an architecture that lends itself to further additions. Here, we were presented with the chance to rebuild a product better than anything else in the travel industry.


Initial Meetings
Our initial conversations revealed our concerns and current constraints, our advantages (over our competitors) and ideas for eventual success. During these meetings we came to understand and consequently align on the core value of the product. We were going to spend several months integrating, scaling and gradually enhancing this experience, therefore creating a tangible vision and highlighting its significance was of utmost importance.
Distilling our Direction
After myriads of casual and formal meetings with both the product and business teams, we had compiled a list of handy notes. Our developers knew enough to begin working on the app architecture, and it was up to us, PM and designer, to begin distilling the design direction.

With lots of notes and scribbles explaining our experience, we concluded on the two main user flows constituting the overall experience. The first, seat selection, was obvious, we needed a seat maps UI after all. The second dealt with figuring out where Seat Maps was going to exist within the checkout ecosystem. We prioritized the Seat Maps UI and left the wiring up as conversation we’d gradually have with the Checkout Team.
At the time we started conceptualizing seat maps, the Checkout app was fairly new and was still being tested. Having this interface in flux posed a challenge, as we couldn’t propose any large architectural change to fit seat maps into the flow. We opted for a simple launch from a link on the first form in the passenger screen.

Going Responsive
After some back and forth with our team, we opted for going responsive. The biggest challenge in creating responsive seat representations is standardizing seat sizes and paddings at different widths for different configurations.

For instance, a 40-pixel by 40-pixel seat works perfectly on an iPhone 4, only if the representation is less than or equal to a 3-3 (three rows by three rows). Adding one more row to the representation (i.e. 2-3-2) would require us to drop the seat size to 30-pixels and adjust the padding accordingly.
Visual Design
Random symbols, incongruent colors, and static content characterize most seat maps interfaces at the moment. My objective in deriving a visual language for our user interface was to keep things simple and self-explanatory. Where other interfaces use a lot of static text and strong colors, I focus on keeping relevant information in notorious places and let the colors speak for themselves.
Static vs. Reactive
In deciding to keep the interface clean, I opted for having an interface that responds with suggestive forms of validation: popups, toast messages and modals. We are not accustomed to this at Priceline, and as far as I know, neither are our competitors.
Conclusion
We have created a competitive seat maps experience with potential to grow even more. Currently, we are brainstorming the introduction for paid seats (as seen in the prototype above). The challenge in offering paid seats, as an OTA, is accommodating for various airlines with one consistent paradigm.
